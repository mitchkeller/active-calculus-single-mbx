Solution B.6.9.1.

\(r=2\cos\theta\) is a circle with radius 1 centered on (1,0), while \(r=1 + \cos\theta\) is a cardioid that entirely encloses the circle. Therefore, to find the area, we subtract the area enclosed by the circle from that enclosed by the cardioid. The cardioid, \(r=1 + \cos\theta\text{,}\) is traced out as \(\theta\) goes from \(\theta=0\) to \(\theta=2\pi\text{.}\) Thus, remembering that the area enclosed by a region lying within the polar equation \(r = f(\theta)\) is Area\(= \int_\alpha^\beta\,{1\over2}(f(\theta))^2\,d\theta\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \hbox{Area of cardioid } = \int_0^{2\pi}{1\over2}(1 + \cos\theta)^2\,d\theta. \end{equation*}
The circle is traced out as \(\theta\) goes from \(\theta=0\) to \(\theta=\pi\text{,}\) so its area is
\begin{equation*} \hbox{Area of circle }= \int_0^{\pi}{1\over2}(2\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta. \end{equation*}
Of course, because the circle has radius 1, we know this second integral will evaluate to \(\pi\text{.}\) We can find the area of the cardioid,
\begin{equation*} \int_0^{2\pi}{1\over2}(1 + \cos\theta)^2\,d\theta = {1\over2}\int_0^{2\pi} (1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta)\,d\theta, \end{equation*}
numerically by using a calculator, or by parts, or using a table of integrals, to find that the area of the cardioid is \(A = {3\pi\over 2}\text{.}\) Thus, the area enclosed by the cardioid that lies outside of the circle is given by \(A = {3\pi\over 2} - \pi = {\pi\over 2}\text{.}\)
