The values of the exponential function \(e^x\) and the quadratic \((x-c)^2\) are positive for \(x \neq c\text{,}\) so the sign of \(q'(x) = -\frac{x-c+1}{(x-c)^2e^x}\) depends entirely on the sign of \(x-(c-1)\text{.}\) When \(x \lt c-1\) we see that \(q'(x) \gt 0\) and when \(x \gt c-1\) we have \(q'(x) \lt 0\text{.}\) Thus, \(q\) is increasing on \((-\infty, c-1)\) and is decreasing on \((c-1,c)\text{,}\) so it follows that \(q(c-1)\) is a relative maximum value. This value is a local maximum, since the value of \(q\) can be made as large as we like as shown in part (b).
