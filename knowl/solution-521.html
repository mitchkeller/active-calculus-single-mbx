Solution 8.4.3.1.

Let \(f(x) = e^x\text{.}\)
  1. For \(f(x) = e^x\text{,}\) we know that \(f'(x) = e^x\text{,}\) and thus \(f^{(k)}(x) = e^x\) for every natural number \(k\text{.}\) Thus, \(f^{(k)}(0) = e^0 = 1\) for every natural number \(k\text{.}\)
  2. With \(f^{(k)}(0) = 1\) for every natural number \(k\text{,}\) in the the Taylor series, \(T_f(x)\) centered at \(a = 0\) of \(f(x) = e^x\text{,}\) we have \(c_k = \frac{f^{(k)}(0)}{k!} = \frac{1}{k!}\text{.}\) Thus
    \begin{equation*} T_f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k!} x^k = 1 + x + \frac{1}{2!}x^2 + \frac{1}{3!}x^3 + \cdots + \frac{1}{n!}x^n + \cdots \end{equation*}
  3. Observe that
    \begin{equation*} r_n(x) = \frac{\frac{1}{(n+1)!}x^{n+1}}{\frac{1}{n!}x^n} = \frac{n!}{(n+1)!} \cdot \frac{x^{n+1}}{x^n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\frac{n!}{(n+1)!} = \frac{1}{n+1}\) and \(\frac{x^{n+1}}{x^n} = x\text{,}\) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} r_n(x) = \frac{1}{n+1} \cdot x\text{.} \end{equation*}
  4. Now we are ready to take the limit of \(r_n(x)\) as \(n \to \infty\) and apply the Ratio Test. Since we fix the value of \(x\) and know that \(\frac{1}{n+1} \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} r(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} r_n(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n+1} \cdot x = 0 \cdot x = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(|r(x)| = |0| = 0\text{,}\) and \(|r(x)| \lt 1\) for every value of \(x\text{.}\) By the Ratio Test, we now know that \(T_f(x)\) converges for every real number \(x\text{.}\)
  5. Plotting \(f(x) = e^x\text{,}\) \(T_{10}(x)\text{,}\) and \(T_{20}(x)\) on the same axes, we observe that \(f(x)\) and \(T_{10}(x)\) are almost indistinguishable on the interval \(-4 \lt x \lt 4\text{,}\) and that when we consider \(T_{20}(x)\text{,}\) that interval widens to almost \(-8 \lt x \lt 8\text{.}\) For instance,
    \begin{equation*} f(-4) - T_{10}(-4) \approx -0.0784 \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} f(-8) - T_{20}(-8) \approx -0.1320\text{.} \end{equation*}
