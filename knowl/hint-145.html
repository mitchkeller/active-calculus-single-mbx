Hint 9.3.2.1.1.

The integral involves \(\tan u\) and \(\sec u\) where the exponent of \(\tan u\) is odd and positive. So we rewrite in order to have a copy of \(\sec(14 x) \tan(14 x)\) and use a trigonometric identity to turn the remaining even powers of \(\tan(14 x)\) into an expression of \(\sec^2(14 x)\text{.}\) Then use u-substitution with \(u= \sec(14 x)\text{.}\)
Thus the integral can be rewritten as
\begin{equation*} \int 2\tan^{3}\!\left(14x\right)\sec^{3}\!\left(14x\right) \, dx = 2 \int \tan^2(14 x) \sec^2(14 x) \sec(14 x) \tan(14 x) \, dx \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = 2 \int \bigg( \sec^2(14 x)-1 \bigg) \sec^2(14 x) \sec(14 x) \tan(14 x) \, dx \end{equation*}
Now let \(u= \sec(14 x)\text{.}\)
Letting \(u= \sec(14 x)\) means that \(du= 14 \sec(14 x)\tan(14 x) \, dx\text{,}\) and thus \(dx= \frac{du}{14\sec(14 x)\tan(14 x)}\text{.}\)
So the integral turns into
\begin{equation*} = 2 \int \bigg( u^2-1 \bigg) u^2 \sec(14 x) \tan(14 x) \frac{du}{14\sec(14 x)\tan(14 x)} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = \frac{2}{14} \int \bigg( u^2-1 \bigg) u^2 \, du = \frac{2}{14} \int \bigg( u^4-u^2 \bigg) \, du \end{equation*}
And each part can be integrated with power rule. Don't forget to plug back in what \(u\) equals!
