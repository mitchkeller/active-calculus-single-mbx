The integral involves \(\tan u\) and \(\sec u\) where the exponent of \(\sec u\) is even and positive. So we rewrite in order to have one copy of \(\sec^2(3 x)\) for the derivative, and use a trigonometric identity to turn the remaining even powers of \(\sec(3 x)\) into an expression of \(\tan^2(3 x)\text{.}\) Then use u-substitution with \(u= \tan(3 x)\text{.}\)
Thus the integral can be rewritten as
\begin{equation*} \int 12\tan^{4}\!\left(3x\right)\sec^{4}\!\left(3x\right) \, dx = 12 \int \tan^4(3 x) \sec^2(3 x) \sec^2(3 x) \, dx \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = 12 \int \tan^4(3 x) \bigg( \tan^2(3 x)+1 \bigg) \sec^2(3 x) \, dx \end{equation*}
Now let \(u= \tan(3 x)\text{.}\)
Letting \(u= \tan(3 x)\) means that \(du= 3 \sec^2(3 x) \, dx\text{,}\) and thus \(dx= \frac{du}{3\sec^2(3 x)}\text{.}\)
So the integral turns into
\begin{equation*} = 12 \int u^4\bigg( u^2+1 \bigg) \sec^2(3 x) \frac{du}{3\sec^2(3 x)} \end{equation*}
Get it down to just \(u\)'s, multiply out, integrate, and don't forget to plug back in what \(u\) equals.
