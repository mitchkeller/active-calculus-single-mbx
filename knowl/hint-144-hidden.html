The integral involves \(\sin u\) and \(\cos u\) where the exponent of \(\cos u\) is an odd, positive integer, namely, 3. So we factor out a copy of \(\cos(-6 x)\) and use the identity \(\sin^2 u + \cos^2 u = 1\) to turn the remaining \(\cos^2(-6 x)\) into an expression involving \(\sin^2(-6 x)\text{.}\)
Thus the integral can be rewritten as
\begin{equation*} \int \sin^{2}\!\left(-6x\right)\cos^{3}\!\left(-6x\right) \, dx = \int \sin^{2} (-6 x) \cos^2(-6 x) \cos(-6 x) \, dx \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = \int \sin^{2} (-6 x) \bigg(1-\sin^2(-6 x)\bigg) \cos(-6 x) \, dx \end{equation*}
and we use u-substitution.
Letting \(u= \sin (-6 x)\) so \(du = -6 \cos (-6 x) \, dx\) and thus \(\displaystyle dx = \frac{ du}{-6 \cos (-6 x)}\text{.}\)
This turns the integral into
\begin{equation*} \int u^{2} (1-u^2) \cos(-6 x) \, \frac{ du}{-6 \cos (-6 x)} = \frac{1}{-6}\int u^{2} (1-u^2) \, du \end{equation*}
which can be multiplied out, and then each part integrated by power rule.
