This integration can be done because we know that the derivative with respect to \(x\) of \(\cos(x)\) is \(-\sin(x)\text{.}\) So we obtain a \(-\sin(x)\) inside the integral, and we can then integrate to obtain \(\cos(x)\text{.}\) Of course, since there isn’t a minus sign inside the integral, we’ll have to multiply inside and outside by \(-1\) (which doesn’t change the value of course):
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int_0^\pi 10 \sin(x) dx \amp = -10 \int_0^\pi (-\sin(x)) dx \\ \amp = -10 \left( \cos(x) \bigg|_0^\pi \right) \\ \amp = -10 ( \cos(\pi) - \cos(0) ) = -10 (-1-1) = 2 \cdot 10 = 20 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
