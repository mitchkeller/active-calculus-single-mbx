Let \(u=\sec\mathopen{}\left(10x\right)\text{.}\) Then we take the derivative of both sides to get
\begin{equation*} du = 10\sec\mathopen{}\left(10x\right)\tan\mathopen{}\left(10x\right) \, dx \end{equation*}
You can do the next step in different ways, but one way is to solve for \(dx\text{.}\) Since \(du = 10\sec\mathopen{}\left(10x\right)\tan\mathopen{}\left(10x\right) \, dx\text{,}\) we divide over to get that
\begin{equation*} dx=\frac{1}{10\sec\mathopen{}\left(10x\right)\tan\mathopen{}\left(10x\right)} \, du \end{equation*}
Then we substitute back into the original integral.
\begin{equation*} \int \frac{2 \sec(10 x)\tan(10 x)}{\sec^{9}(10 x)} \, dx = \int \frac{2 \sec(10 x)\tan(10 x)}{u^{9}} \frac{1}{10\sec\mathopen{}\left(10x\right)\tan\mathopen{}\left(10x\right)} \, du \end{equation*}
Which simplifies to
\begin{equation*} \int \frac{0.2}{u^{9}}du \end{equation*}
Now, this is one of our basic integrals because we can write it as \(\displaystyle \int 0.2 u^{-9} \, du\) and thus we can integrate it. Constants in front just stay multiplied so we get
\begin{equation*} \int 0.2 u^{-9} \, du = \frac{0.2}{-8} u^{-8}+C \end{equation*}
Finally, make sure you put it back in terms of the original variable, which in this case is ’x’. So we substitute back in for ’u’, which was \(\sec\mathopen{}\left(10x\right)\text{.}\) Thus the final answer to this integral is
\begin{equation*} \int \frac{2 \sec(10 x)\tan(10 x)}{\sec^{9}(10 x)} \, dx = -0.025\mathopen{}\left(\sec\mathopen{}\left(10x\right)\right)^{-8}+C \end{equation*}
