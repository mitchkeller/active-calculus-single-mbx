The integral involves \(\tan u\) and \(\sec u\) where the exponent of \(\tan u\) is odd and positive. So we rewrite in order to have a copy of \(\sec(-13 x) \tan(-13 x)\) and use a trigonometric identity to turn the remaining even powers of \(\tan(-13 x)\) into an expression of \(\sec^2(-13 x)\text{.}\) Then use u-substitution with \(u= \sec(-13 x)\text{.}\)
Thus the integral can be rewritten as
\begin{equation*} \int 3\tan^{3}\mathopen{}\left(-13x\right)\sec^{3}\mathopen{}\left(-13x\right) \, dx = 3 \int \tan^2(-13 x) \sec^2(-13 x) \sec(-13 x) \tan(-13 x) \, dx \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = 3 \int \bigg( \sec^2(-13 x)-1 \bigg) \sec^2(-13 x) \sec(-13 x) \tan(-13 x) \, dx \end{equation*}
Now let \(u= \sec(-13 x)\text{.}\)
Letting \(u= \sec(-13 x)\) means that \(du= -13 \sec(-13 x)\tan(-13 x) \, dx\text{,}\) and thus \(dx= \frac{du}{-13\sec(-13 x)\tan(-13 x)}\text{.}\)
So the integral turns into
\begin{equation*} = 3 \int \bigg( u^2-1 \bigg) u^2 \sec(-13 x) \tan(-13 x) \frac{du}{-13\sec(-13 x)\tan(-13 x)} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = \frac{3}{-13} \int \bigg( u^2-1 \bigg) u^2 \, du = \frac{3}{-13} \int \bigg( u^4-u^2 \bigg) \, du \end{equation*}
