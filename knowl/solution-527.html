Solution 8.2.4.1.

Let \(f(x) = \ln(x)\) and consider the degree \(4\) Taylor polynomial approximation
\begin{equation*} T_4(x) = c_0 + c_1 (x-2) + c_2 (x-2)^2 + c_3(x-2)^3 + c_4(x-2)^4\text{.} \end{equation*}
  2. We see that
    \begin{align*} T_4(x) =\mathstrut \amp c_0 + c_1 (x-2) + c_2 (x-2)^2 + c_3(x-2)^3 + c_4(x-2)^4\\ =\mathstrut \amp \ln(2) + \frac{1}{2}(x-2) - \frac{1}{2! 2^2} (x-2)^2 + \frac{(-2)(-1)}{3! 2^3}(x-2)^3 + \frac{(-3)(-2)(-1)}{4! 2^4}(x-2)^4\\ =\mathstrut \amp \ln(2) + \frac{1}{2}(x-2) - \frac{1}{2 \cdot 2^2} (x-2)^2 + \frac{1}{3 \cdot 2^3}(x-2)^3 - \frac{1}{4 \cdot 2^4}(x-2)^4\text{.} \end{align*}
  3. Plotting \(f(x) = \ln(x)\text{,}\) \(T_1(x) = \ln(2) + \frac{1}{2}(x - 2)\text{,}\) and \(T_4(x)\text{,}\) we observe, as expected, that \(T_4(x)\) provides a much better approximation of \(f(x)\) near \(a = 2\) and on a wider interval.
  4. Using a slider in Desmos, we see that\(|f(x) - T_4(x)| \lt 0.1\) for approximately \(0.58 \lt x \lt 3.95\text{.}\)
  5. Based on the pattern in Table 8.2.24 and \(T_4(x) = \ln(2) + \frac{1}{2}(x-2) - \frac{1}{2 \cdot 2^2} (x-2)^2 + \frac{1}{3 \cdot 2^3}(x-2)^3 - \frac{1}{4 \cdot 2^4}(x-2)^4\text{,}\) we expect that
    \begin{equation*} T_5(x) = \ln(2) + \frac{1}{2}(x-2) - \frac{1}{2 \cdot 2^2} (x-2)^2 + \frac{1}{3 \cdot 2^3}(x-2)^3 - \frac{1}{4 \cdot 2^4}(x-2)^4 + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2^5 (x-2)^5} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} T_6(x) = \ln(2) + \frac{1}{2}(x-2) - \frac{1}{2 \cdot 2^2} (x-2)^2 + \frac{1}{3 \cdot 2^3}(x-2)^3 - \frac{1}{4 \cdot 2^4}(x-2)^4 + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2^5 (x-2)^5} - \frac{1}{6 \cdot 2^6 (x-2)^6}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    When we increase the degree to \(n = 5\text{,}\) we see that \(|f(x) - T_5(x)| \lt 0.1\) for roughly \(0.48 \lt x \lt 4\text{,}\) and if we go to \(n = 6\text{,}\) \(|f(x) - T_6(x)| \lt 0.1\) for about \(0.42 \lt x lt 4\text{.}\) This is similar to the Taylor approximations centered at \(a = 1\) in Activity 8.2.3 in that there’s an upper bound on the approximation interval and that the width of the interval doesn’t increase much with the higher degree, but it’s different in that the interval of accuracy is wider. It seems that by moving to \(a = 2\text{,}\) which is further away from the asymptote at \(x = 0\text{,}\) we can get approximations of \(ln(x)\) that are good all the way up to \(x = 4\text{.}\)
