Hint 5.4.7.3.1.

We pick \(u\) and \(dv\) so that \(dv\) can be integrated, and then (ideally) \(u\) gets simpler.
We do not know how to easily integrate \(\ln\!\left(-6x\right)\text{,}\) so that must be \(u\text{.}\)
Then everything else in the integral must equal \(dv\text{.}\) So \(dv =x^{8}dx\text{.}\)
Since \(u=\ln\!\left(-6x\right)\) we take the derivative to get that \(du= \frac{1}{x}dx =\frac{1}{x} \, dx\text{.}\)
Since \(dv =x^{8}dx\) we integrate to get that \(v= \frac{1}{9} x^{9}\)
Next, remember that the integration by parts formula says that
\begin{equation*} \int u \, dv = u\cdot v - \int v \, du \end{equation*}
So, substituting in what we already know are \(u, \ v,\ du\) and \(dv\text{,}\) we get that
\begin{equation*} \int x^{8} \ln\!\left(-6x\right) \, dx = \ln\!\left(-6x\right) \cdot \frac{1}{9} x^{9} - \int \frac{1}{9} x^{9} \cdot \frac{1}{x} \, dx \end{equation*}
Now cancel the x's in that last integral, and it's a basic integral (power rule) so you can evaluate it.
