For example, if we have a nonnegative velocity function that is not constant, over a short time interval \(\Delta t\) we know that the distance traveled is approximately \(v(t) \Delta t\text{,}\) since \(v(t)\) is almost constant on a small interval. Similarly, if we are thinking about the area under a nonnegative function \(f\) whose value is changing, on a short interval \(\Delta x\) the area under the curve is approximately the area of the rectangle whose height is \(f(x)\) and whose width is \(\Delta x\text{:}\) \(f(x) \Delta x\text{.}\) Both of these principles are represented visually in FigureÂ 6.3.1.
