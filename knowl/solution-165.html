One way to estimate the graph of \(y = f^{-1}(x)\) is to plot several \((x,y)\) points on the graph of \(y = f(x)\) and then plot the corresponding points \((y,x)\) with the coordinates reversed. Doing so and recalling that the graph of \(y = f^{-1}(x)\) is the reflection of the graph of \(y = f(x)\) across the line \(y = x\text{,}\) we see the following figure.

  3. Because the original function passes through the point \((1,-1)\text{,}\) the inverse function passes through \((-1,1)\text{.}\) Moreover, since the tangent line to the original function at \((1,-1)\) has slope \(f'(1) \approx 2\text{,}\) and the tangent line to the inverse function at \((-1,1)\) corresponds to the reflection of the original tangent line across the line \(y=x\text{,}\) it follows that \((f^{-1})'(-1) \approx 1/2\text{.}\) This can also be deduced from the ``derivative of an inverse function'' rule, whereby \((f^{-1})'(-1) = \frac{1}{f'(1)}\text{.}\)
