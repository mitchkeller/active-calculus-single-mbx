Solution 8.5.5.1.

  1. Since \(\sin(x) = x - \frac{1}{3!}x^3 + \frac{1}{5!}x^7 - \cdots\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \sin(1) = 1 - \frac{1}{3!}1^3 + \frac{1}{5!}1^7 - \frac{1}{7!}1^9 + \cdots \end{equation*}
    so that, for example,
    \begin{equation*} \sin(1) \approx 1 - \frac{1}{3!} + \frac{1}{5!} - \frac{1}{7!}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This approximation has error at most \(\frac{1}{9!}\) since that's the next term in the sum. Since \(\frac{1}{9!} \approx 0.00000276\text{,}\) we are even more accurate than required. Note that since \(\frac{1}{7!} \approx 0.000198\text{,}\) we can't stop after the third term and indeed need the four above. Thus,
    \begin{equation*} \sin(1) \approx 1 - \frac{1}{3!} + \frac{1}{5!} - \frac{1}{7!} = \frac{4241}{5040} = 0.8414682539682539\ldots \end{equation*}
    If we as a computational device the value of \(\sin(1)\text{,}\) we get \(\sin(1) = 0.84147098\ldots\text{.}\)
  2. Since
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^1 e^{-x^2} \, dx = 1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!} - \frac{1}{7 \cdot 3!} + \cdots + \frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1) n!} + \cdots\text{,} \end{equation*}
    we can truncate the alternating series to get a good estimate of the integral. We note that \(\frac{1}{11 \cdot 5!} = \frac{1}{1320} = 0.0007575\ldots\text{,}\) and the next term after this one is less than \(0.0001\text{,}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^1 e^{-x^2} \, dx \approx 1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!} - \frac{1}{7 \cdot 3!} + \frac{1}{9 \cdot 4!} - \frac{1}{11 \cdot 5!} = \frac{31049}{41580} = 0.7467291967291967291\ldots \end{equation*}
    A computational device reports that \(\int_0^1 e^{-x^2} \, dx \approx 0.746824\text{.}\)
  3. We find that
    \begin{equation*} \cos(x^2) = 1 - \frac{1}{2!}x^4 + \frac{1}{4!}x^8 - \frac{1}{6!}x^{12} + \cdots\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^1 \cos(x^2) \, dx = \left. x - \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!}x^5 + \frac{1}{9 \cdot 4!}x^9 - \frac{1}{13 \cdot 6!}x^{13} + \cdots \right|_0^1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^1 \cos(x^2) \, dx = 1 - \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!} + \frac{1}{9 \cdot 4!} - \frac{1}{13 \cdot 6!} + \cdots \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\frac{1}{13 \cdot 6!} = \frac{1}{9360} \approx 0.0001068\text{,}\) the next term is less than \(0.0001\) and thus we have
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^1 \cos(x^2) \, dx \approx 1 - \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!} + \frac{1}{9 \cdot 4!} - \frac{1}{13 \cdot 6!} = \frac{25399}{28080} = 0.904522792022792022792\ldots \text{.} \end{equation*}
    A computational device reports that \(\int_0^1 \cos(x^2) \, dx \approx 0.914524\text{.}\)
  4. If we let \(x = 1\) in the series
    \begin{equation*} x - \frac{1}{2}x^2 + \frac{1}{3}x^3 - \cdots + (-1)^{n-1} \frac{1}{n} x^n + \cdots\text{,} \end{equation*}
    we see that we get the converging alternating series
    \begin{equation*} 1 - \frac{1}{2} \cdot 1^2 + \frac{1}{3} \cdot 1^3 - \cdots + (-1)^{n-1} \frac{1}{n} \cdot 1^n + \cdots \end{equation*}
    by the Alternating Series Theorem. Indeed, since \(\ln(1+x) = x - \frac{1}{2}x^2 + \frac{1}{3}x^3 - \cdots + (-1)^{n-1} \frac{1}{n} x^n + \cdots\text{,}\) it follows that the exact sum of the series
    \begin{equation*} 1 - \frac{1}{2} \cdot 1^2 + \frac{1}{3} \cdot 1^3 - \cdots + (-1)^{n-1} \frac{1}{n} \cdot 1^n + \cdots \end{equation*}
    is \(\ln(2)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \ln(2) = 1 - \frac{1}{2} \cdot 1^2 + \frac{1}{3} \cdot 1^3 - \cdots + (-1)^{n-1} \frac{1}{n} \cdot 1^n + \cdots\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is an example of a Taylor series that converges at one of the endpoints of the interval of convergence.
