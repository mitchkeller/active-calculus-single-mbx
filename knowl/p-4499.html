By the chain rule and treating \(A\text{,}\) \(c\text{,}\) and \(k\) as constants, we find that
\begin{equation*} L'(t) = A(-1)(1+ce^{-kt})^{-2} ce^{-kt}(-k) = Acke^{-kt}(1+ce^{-kt})^{-2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since \(A\text{,}\) \(c\text{,}\) and \(k\) are all positive and \(e^{-kt} \gt 0\) for all values of \(t\text{,}\) it is apparent that \(L'(t)\) is never zero, and indeed is positive for every value of \(t\text{.}\) Thus, \(L\) is an always increasing function.
