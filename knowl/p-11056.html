We let \(f(x) = \ln(1+x)\) and work to determine whether or not the Taylor series is geometric in order to investigate the \(x\)-values for which the series appears to converge. In ExampleÂ 8.2.6, we found that the general degree \(n\) Taylor polynomial centered at \(a = 0\) for \(f(x) = \ln(1+x)\) is
\begin{equation*} T_n(x) = x - \frac{1}{2}x^2 + \frac{1}{3}x^3 - \cdots + (-1)^{n-1}\frac{1}{n}x^n\text{.} \end{equation*}
This pattern thus allows us to see that the Taylor series for \(f(x)\text{,}\) centered at \(a = 0\text{,}\) is
\begin{align*} T_f(x) =\mathstrut \amp \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} (-1)^{k-1}\frac{1}{k}x^k\\ =\mathstrut \amp x - \frac{1}{2}x^2 + \frac{1}{3}x^3 - \cdots + (-1)^{n-1}\frac{1}{n}x^n + \cdots\text{.} \end{align*}
Unlike the Taylor series for the function \(\frac{1}{1-x}\text{,}\) the Taylor series for \(f(x) = \ln(1+x)\) turns out to not be geometric, so we introduce some new ideas to think about the \(x\)-values for which we expect the series to converge.
