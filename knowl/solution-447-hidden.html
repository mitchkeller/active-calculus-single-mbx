A. With one step of \(\Delta t = 1\text{,}\) we have \(B(1) \approx 800 + (1)(0.07)(800) = 856\text{.}\) B. With two steps of \(\Delta t = 0.5\text{,}\) we have \(B(0.5) \approx 800 + (0.5)(0.07)(800) = 828\text{,}\) \(B(1) \approx 828 + (0.5)(0.07)(828) = 856.98\text{.}\) Note that we can simplify this some: \(B(0.5) \approx (1 + \frac{0.07}{2}) (800)\text{,}\) so that \(B(1) \approx B(0.5) + \frac{0.07}{2} B(0.5) = (1 + \frac{0.07}{2})^2 (800)\text{.}\) C. With four steps of \(\Delta t = 0.25\text{,}\) we have, following the pattern we saw in (B), \(B(0.25) \approx 800 + (0.25)(0.07)(800) = (1 + \frac{0.07}{4}) (800)\text{,}\) \(B(0.5) \approx B(0.25) + (0.25)(0.07) B(0.25) = (1 + \frac{0.07}{4})^2 (800)\text{,}\) \(B(0.75) \approx B(0.5) + (0.25)(0.07) B(0.5) = (1 + \frac{0.07}{4})^3 (800)\text{,}\) and \(B(1) \approx B(0.75) + (0.25)(0.07) B(0.75) = (1 + \frac{0.07}{4})^4 (800) = 857.487\text{.}\) D. In part (A), we get our approximation by making a single increment, \(\Delta B\text{,}\) where \(\Delta B\) is just \(0.07 B\text{.}\) If we think in terms of interest, \(\Delta B\) is just like getting one end of the year interest payment. Since \(\Delta B\) is 0.07 times the balance \(B\text{,}\) it is like getting 56 dollars. Part (B) is equivalent to computing the final amount in an account that begins with 800 dollars and earns 56 dollars twice annually. Each step is like computing the interest after 6 months. When \(t=0.5\text{,}\) for example, the interest is \(\Delta B=(0.07 B)\cdot\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) and we add this to 800 dollars to get the new balance. Similarly, part (C) is equivalent to the final amount in an account that has an initial balance of 800 dollars and earns 56 dollars compounded quarterly.
