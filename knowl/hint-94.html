Hint 5.4.7.1.1.

We pick \(u\) and \(dv\) so that \(dv\) can be integrated, while ideally, \(u\) gets simpler.
Both \(x\) and \(\cos\mathopen{}\left(3x\right)\) can be integrated, so we can choose for \(u\) to get simpler.
The derivative of \(x\) is \(1\) (which is of lower degree) while the derivative of \(\cos\mathopen{}\left(3x\right)\) is \(-3 \sin(3 x)\text{,}\) (which is another trig function and not any simpler). Thus, we should let \(u=x\) and \(dv=\cos\mathopen{}\left(3x\right)dx\text{.}\)
Then \(du=dx\) and \(v=\frac{1}{3} \sin( 3 x)\text{.}\)
Next, remember that the integration by parts formula says that
\begin{equation*} \int u \, dv = u\cdot v - \int v \, du \end{equation*}
So, substituting in what we already know are \(u, \ v,\ du\) and \(dv\text{,}\) we get that
\begin{equation*} \int x \cos(3 x) \, dx = x \cdot \frac{1}{3}\sin(3 x) - \int \frac{1}{3}\sin(3 x) \, dx \end{equation*}
Lastly, we integrate the second part, so the answer is
\begin{equation*} \int x \cos(3 x) \, dx = \frac{1}{3} x \sin(3 x) + \frac{1}{9}\cos(3 x)+C \end{equation*}
