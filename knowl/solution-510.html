From the solutions graphed above, or by looking at the slope field, we can see that the equilibrium solution \(P = 1\) is stable. The other equilibrium solution, \(P = 0\) is unstable. Also by looking at the solution curves, we can see that \(P\) is increasing when \(0 \lt P \lt 1\) and decreasing when \(P > 1\text{.}\) In the long run, weâ€™re most likely to start with a non-zero population and thus \(P\to 1\text{.}\) The solution curves with initial populations of less than \(P= 0.5\) have inflection points at \(P=0.5\text{.}\) (This will be demonstrated algebraically below.) At the inflection point, the population is growing fastest. A graph of \(dP/dt\) against \(P\) is shown below.
Since \(\frac{dP}{dt}=5 P - 5 P^2 = 5 P(1 - P)\text{,}\) the graph of \(\frac{dP}{dt}\) against \(P\) is a parabola, opening downward with \(P\) intercepts at 0 and 1. The quantity \(\frac{dP}{dt}\) is positive for \(0 \lt P \lt 1\text{,}\) negative for \(P > 1\) (and \(P \lt 0\)). The quantity \(\frac{dP}{dt}\) is 0 at \(P=0\) and \(P=\text{,}\) and maximum at \(P=0.5\text{.}\) The fact that \(\frac{dP}{dt} =0\) at \(P=0\) and \(P=1\) tells us that these are equilibria. Further, since \(\frac{dP}{dt}> 0\) for \(0 \lt P \lt 1\text{,}\) we see that solution curves starting here will increase toward \(P=1\text{.}\) If the population starts at a value \(P\lt 0.5\text{,}\) it increases at an increasing rate up to \(P=0.5\text{.}\) After this, \(P\) continues to increase, but at a decreasing rate. The fact that \(\frac{dP}{dt}\) has a maximum at \(P=0.5\) tells us that there is a point of inflection when \(P=0.5\text{.}\) Similarly, since \(\frac{dP}{dt} \lt 0\) for \(P > 1\text{,}\) solution curves starting with \(P > 1\) will decrease to \(P = 1\text{.}\) Thus, \(P=1\) is a stable equilibrium.
