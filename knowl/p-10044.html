While we could differentiate \(\arctan(x)\) repeatedly and look for patterns in the derivative values at \(x = 0\) in an attempt to find the Maclaurin series for \(\arctan(x)\) from the definition, it turns out to be far easier to use a known series in an insightful way. In ActivityÂ 8.6.3, we found that

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{1+x^2} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^k x^{2k} \end{equation*}

for \(-1 \lt x \lt 1\text{.}\) Recall that

\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx} \left[ \arctan(x) \right] = \frac{1}{1+x^2}\text{,} \end{equation*}

and therefore

\begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{1+x^2} \ dx = \arctan(x) + C\text{.} \end{equation*}
