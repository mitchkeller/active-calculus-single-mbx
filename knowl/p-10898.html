For \(x\) such that \(|x| \lt 1\text{,}\) we know that the infinite geometric series \(1 + x+ x^2 + \cdots + x^n + \cdots\) converges. Thus, from Equation (8.3.14), it follows
\begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{1}{1-x} \approx 1 + x + x^2 + \cdots + x^n \end{equation*}
for \(x\) near \(a = 0\text{.}\) Moreover, because the infinite series converges, the larger the value of \(n\text{,}\) the better the approximation will be. If we plot the function \(f(x) = \frac{1}{1-x}\) along with several of the polynomials that arise for different choices of \(n\text{,}\) say \(P_1(x) = 1 + x\text{,}\) \(P_4(x) = 1 + x + \cdots + x^4\text{,}\) and \(P_7(x) = 1 + x + \cdots + x^7\text{,}\) we can see the impact of increasing the degree \(n\) in Figure 8.3.6.
