Solution 8.2.4.6.1.

\begin{equation*} g(x) = g(4) + g'(4)(x-4) + \frac{g''(4)}{2!}(x-4)^2 +\frac{g'''(4)}{3!}(x-4)^3+\cdots \end{equation*}
Substituting gives
\begin{equation*} g(x) = 1 + 2(x-4) + \frac{-1}{2!}(x-4)^2 +\frac{-4}{3!}(x-4)^3+\cdots \end{equation*}
(a) The degree 2 Taylor polynomial, \(T_2 (x)\text{,}\) is obtained by truncating after the \((x-4)^2\) term:
\begin{equation*} T_2(x) = 1+2\!\left(x-4\right)+\frac{1}{2!}\!\left(-1\right)\!\left(x-4\right)^{2}. \end{equation*}
(b) The degree 3 Taylor polynomial, \(T_3 (x)\text{,}\) is obtained by truncating after the \((x-4)^3\) term:
\begin{equation*} T_3 (x) = 1+2\!\left(x-4\right)+\frac{1}{2!}\!\left(-1\right)\!\left(x-4\right)^{2}+\frac{1}{3!}\!\left(-4\right)\!\left(x-4\right)^{3} \end{equation*}
(c) Substitute \(x=3.9\) into the Taylor polynomial of degree 2:
\begin{equation*} T_2(4.9) = 1+2\!\left(3.9-4\right)+\frac{1}{2!}\!\left(-1\right)\!\left(3.9-4\right)^{2} = 0.795. \end{equation*}
From the Taylor polynomial of degree 3, we obtain
\begin{equation*} T_3(4.9) = 1+2\!\left(3.9-4\right)+\frac{1}{2!}\!\left(-1\right)\!\left(3.9-4\right)^{2}+\frac{1}{3!}\!\left(-4\right)\!\left(3.9-4\right)^{3} = 0.795667. \end{equation*}
