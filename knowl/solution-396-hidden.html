Since the density of water is \(\rho\) lb/ft\({}^3\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \hbox{Weight of the slice }\approx\rho(70 \cdot 30 \cdot \Delta h) \hbox{ lbs}. \end{equation*}
The distance to lift the slice of water at height \(h\) ft is \(10 - h\) ft, so the work to move one slice, \(\Delta W\text{,}\) is
\begin{equation*} \Delta W = \rho \cdot \hbox{Volume} \cdot \hbox{Distance lifted} \approx \rho(70 \cdot 30 \cdot \Delta h)(10 - h) = 2100 \rho (10 - h) \Delta h\hbox{ ft-lb}. \end{equation*}
The work done, \(W\text{,}\) to pump all the water is the sum of the work done on the pieces:
\begin{equation*} W \approx \Sigma\, 2100 \rho(10-h)\Delta h. \end{equation*}
As \(\Delta h \rightarrow 0\text{,}\) we obtain a definite integral. Since \(h\) varies from \(h = 0\) to \(h = 8\) and \(\rho=62.4\hbox{ lb/ft}{}^3\text{,}\) the total work is:
\begin{equation*} W = \int_0^{8} 131040 (10-h)dh = 131040 \left(10 h - {h^2\over 2}\right)\bigg|_0^{8} = 131040 (48) = 6289920\, \hbox{lbf}. \end{equation*}
