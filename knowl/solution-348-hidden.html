Let \(u=\cot\mathopen{}\left(-2x\right)\text{.}\) Then we take the derivative of both sides to get
\begin{equation*} du = 2\csc^{2}\mathopen{}\left(-2x\right) \, dx \end{equation*}
You can do the next step in different ways, but one way is to solve for \(dx\text{.}\) Since \(du = 2\csc^{2}\mathopen{}\left(-2x\right) \, dx\text{,}\) we divide over to get that
\begin{equation*} dx=\frac{1}{2\csc^{2}\mathopen{}\left(-2x\right)} \, du \end{equation*}
Then we substitute back into the original integral.
\begin{equation*} \int 6 \csc^2(-2 x) e^{\cot(-2 x)} \, dx = \int 6 \csc^2(-2 x) e^{u} \frac{1}{2\csc^{2}\mathopen{}\left(-2x\right)} \, du \end{equation*}
Which simplifies to
\begin{equation*} \int 3e^{u}du \end{equation*}
Now, this is one of our basic integrals, and we can integrate it. Constants in front just stay multiplied so we get
\begin{equation*} \int 3e^{u}du = 3e^{u}+C \end{equation*}
Finally, make sure you put it back in terms of the original variable, which in this case is ’x’. So we substitute back in for ’u’, which was \(\cot\mathopen{}\left(-2x\right)\text{.}\) Thus the final answer to this integral is
\begin{equation*} \int 6 \csc^2(-2 x) e^{\cot(-2 x)} \, dx = 3e^{\cot\mathopen{}\left(-2x\right)}+C \end{equation*}
