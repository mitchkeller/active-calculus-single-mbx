Solution 9.3.2.2.1.

The integral involves \(\tan u\) and \(\sec u\) where the exponent of \(\sec u\) is even and positive. So we rewrite in order to have one copy of \(\sec^2(3 x)\) for the derivative, and use a trigonometric identity to turn the remaining even powers of \(\sec(3 x)\) into an expression of \(\tan^2(3 x)\text{.}\)
Then let \(u= \tan(3 x)\text{,}\) which means that \(du= 3 \sec^2(3 x) \, dx\text{,}\) and thus \(dx= \frac{du}{3\sec^2(3 x)}\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int 12\tan^{4}\!\left(3x\right)\sec^{4}\!\left(3x\right) \, dx \amp = 12 \int \tan^4(3 x) \sec^2(3 x) \sec^2(3 x) \, dx \\\\ \amp = 12 \int \tan^4(3 x) \bigg( \tan^2(3 x)+1 \bigg) \sec^2(3 x) \, dx \\\\ \amp = 12 \int u^4\bigg( u^2+1 \bigg) \sec^2(3 x) \frac{du}{3\sec^2(3 x)} \\\\ \amp = \frac{12}{3} \int u^4\bigg( u^2+1 \bigg) \, du \\\\ \amp = \frac{12}{3} \int \bigg( u^6+u^4 \bigg) \, du \\\\ \amp = \frac{12}{3} \bigg[\frac{ u^7}{7}+\frac{u^5}{5}\bigg]+C \\\\ \amp = \frac{12}{3} \bigg[\frac{ \tan^7(3 x)}{7}+\frac{\tan^5(3 x)}{5}\bigg]+C \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
The correct trigonometric identity used is B.
