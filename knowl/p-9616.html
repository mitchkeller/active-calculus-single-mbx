  1. We see that
    \begin{align*} r_n(x) =\mathstrut \amp \frac{ \frac{1}{ (n+1) \cdot 2^{n+1} } (x-1)^{n+1} } { \frac{1}{n \cdot 2^n} (x-1)^n }\\ =\mathstrut \amp \frac{n}{n+1} \cdot \frac{2^n}{2^{n+1}} \cdot \frac{(x-1)^{n+1}}{(x-1)^n}\\ =\mathstrut \amp \frac{n}{n+1} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot (x-1) \end{align*}
  2. For \(r(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} r_n(x)\text{,}\) we observe that
    \begin{align*} r(x) =\mathstrut \amp \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n}{n+1} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot (x-1) \\ =\mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{2} \cdot (x-1) \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n}{n+1} \\ =\mathstrut \amp \frac{1}{2} \cdot (x-1) \end{align*}
  3. Now we can say that \(|r(x)| \lt 1\) whenever
    \begin{equation*} |\frac{1}{2} \cdot (x-1)| \lt 1\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which implies
    \begin{equation*} |x - 1| \lt 2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(T(x)\) is guaranteed to converge whenever \(1 \lt x \lt 3\text{.}\)
  4. When we plot \(T_{10}(x)\) and \(f(x) = \ln(2) - \ln(3-x)\) on the interval from \(1 \lt x \lt 3\text{,}\) we see that \(T_{10}\) and \(f\) are nearly indistinguishable for a considerable subset of the interval. This suggests that \(T(x)\) is the Taylor serise of \(f(x) = \ln(2) - \ln(3-x)\text{.}\)
