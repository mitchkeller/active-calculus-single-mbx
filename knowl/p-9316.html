The last equality we demonstrated above tells us that

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^n (a_k+b_k) = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k + \sum_{k=1}^{n} b_k\text{.} \end{equation*}

In this equality, we can take the limit of both sides as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\) On the right side, we know that both \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k\) and \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{n} b_k\) exist because the two series converge, and therefore the limit of the sum is the sum of the limits. Hence,

\begin{align*} \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^n (a_k+b_k) &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left( \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k + \sum_{k=1}^{n} b_k \right)\\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k + \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{n} b_k\text{.} \end{align*}

Thus we have shown that

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} (a_k+b_k) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a_k + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} b_k\text{.} \end{equation*}
