1. Finding the first \(6\) derivatives of \(f(x) = \sin(x)\) and evaluating them at \(a = \frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) we see the results in Table 8.2.35. Using the fact that \(c_k = \frac{f^{k}(\frac{pi}{2})}{k!}\text{,}\) we find that the degree \(6\) Taylor polynomial approximation of \(\sin(x)\) centered at \(a = \frac{\pi}{2}\) is
    \begin{equation*} T_6(x) = 1 - \frac{1}{2!}(x-\frac{1}{2})^2 + \frac{1}{4!}(x-\frac{1}{2})^4 - \frac{1}{6!}(x-\frac{1}{2})^6 \end{equation*}
  2. Earlier, we found that the degree \(6\) Taylor polynomial of \(\cos(x)\) centered at \(a = 0\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} P_6(x) = 1 - \frac{1}{2!}x^2 + \frac{1}{4!}x^4 - \frac{1}{6!}x^6\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so the degree \(6\) Taylor polynomial of \(\sin(x)\) centered at \(a = \frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) \(T_6(x)\) appears to be the same as \(P_6(x-\frac{\pi}{2})\text{,}\) the degree \(6\) Taylor polynomial of \(\cos(x)\) centered at \(a = 0\text{,}\) shifted \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) units to the right.
  3. Since \(\sin(x) = \cos(x - \frac{\pi}{2})\text{,}\) this tells us that the sine function is simply a shifted version of the cosine function (the cosine function shifted \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) units to the right), so it makes sense that their Taylor polynomials align as we found in (c), provided that we center the sine function’s approximations at \(a = \frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\)
