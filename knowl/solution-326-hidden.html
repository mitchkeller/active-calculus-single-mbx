\(x=5\text{,}\) we are given that \(f(5)=0\text{.}\) Moving to the left on the interval \(4 \lt x \lt 5\text{,}\) we have \(f'(x) = 3\text{,}\) and we can find \(f(4)\) by knowing that the change in \(f(x)\) over this interval is equal to the area under \(f'(x)\) between \(x=4\) and \(x=5\text{.}\) Thus \(f(5) - f(4) = (3)(1)\text{,}\) so that \(f(4) = f(5) - (1)(3) = -3\text{.}\) Similarly, continuing back to \(x=0\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} f(0) = f(4) - (1)(-1) - (1)(2) - (1)(-1) - (1)(2) = -5 \end{equation*}
Moving to the right from \(x=5\text{,}\) we know that \(f'(x) = -1\) on \(5 \lt x \lt 7\text{.}\) So, as before, \(f(7) = f(5) + (2)(-1) = -2\text{.}\) Similarly, continuing to \(x=7\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} f(7) = f(7) = -2. \end{equation*}
Then, the value of the integral \(\int_0^7 f'(x)\,dx\) is given by the sum
\begin{equation*} \int_0^7 f'(x)\,dx = (2)(1) + (-1)(1) + (2)(1) + (-1)(1) + (3)(1) + (-1)(2) = 3. \end{equation*}
Alternatively, knowing \(f(7)=-2\) and \(f(0)=-5\) and using the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, we have
\begin{equation*} \int_0^7 f'(x)\,dx=f(7)-f(0) = (-2) - (-5) = 3. \end{equation*}
