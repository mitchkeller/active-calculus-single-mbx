A ladder leaning against a wall forms a triangle, with the ladder being the hypotenuse \(z\text{,}\) the wall up to the top of the ladder forming one side \(x\text{,}\) and the floor from the wall to the ladder the third side \(y\text{.}\) Pythagoreanâ€™s theorem relates the lengths of these sides,
\begin{equation*} x^2+y^2=z^2 \end{equation*}
But the hypotenuse is a constant length - the ladder is sliding down the wall, not growing or shrinking, so \(z=14\text{,}\) and \(x^2+y^2=14^2\text{.}\) Thus differentiating both sides gives
\begin{equation*} 2x \frac{dx}{dt} + 2y \frac{dy}{dt} = 0 \end{equation*}
We are told that the top is sliding down the wall, so side \(y\) is decreasing at the rate of 2, which means that \(\frac{dy}{dt}=-2\text{,}\) and we are looking for the rate at which the bottom is moving away from the wall, which is \(\frac{dx}{dt}\text{.}\) We are also given that \(y=13\text{,}\) so we still need to find \(x\text{.}\)
