We pick \(u\) and \(dv\) so that \(dv\) can be integrated, while ideally, \(u\) gets simpler.
Both \(x\) and \(\cos\!\left(4x\right)\) can be integrated, so we can choose for \(u\) to get simpler.
The derivative of \(x\) is \(1\) (which is of lower degree) while the derivative of \(\cos\!\left(4x\right)\) is \(-4 \sin(4 x)\text{,}\) (which is another trig function and not any simpler). Thus, we should let \(u=x\) and \(dv=\cos\!\left(4x\right)dx\text{.}\)
Then \(du=dx\) and \(v=\frac{1}{4} \sin( 4 x)\text{.}\)
Next, remember that the integration by parts formula says that
\begin{equation*} \int u \, dv = u\cdot v - \int v \, du \end{equation*}
So, substituting in what we already know are \(u, \ v,\ du\) and \(dv\text{,}\) we get that
\begin{equation*} \int x \cos(4 x) \, dx = x \cdot \frac{1}{4}\sin(4 x) - \int \frac{1}{4}\sin(4 x) \, dx \end{equation*}
Lastly, we integrate the second part, so the answer is
\begin{equation*} \int x \cos(4 x) \, dx = \frac{1}{4} x \sin(4 x) + \frac{1}{16}\cos(4 x)+C \end{equation*}
