Task 8.5.5.2.a. Part 1.
Note that when the constant in the denominator is not 1, we still can use the geometric series, but we have to multiply by a form of 1 that helps us. We'll use \(\frac{\frac{1}{5}}{\frac{1}{5}}\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(5+x)}=\frac{\frac{1}{5}}{\frac{1}{5}(5+x)}=\frac{\frac{1}{5}}{1+\frac{1}{5}x}=\frac{1}{5}\cdot \frac{1}{1+\frac{1}{5}x} \end{equation*}
And now we can use the geometric series expression to get
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(5+x)}=\frac{1}{5}\cdot \frac{1}{1+\frac{1}{5}x}=\frac{1}{5}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\left(\frac{-1}{5}x\right)^n=\frac{1}{5}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\left(\frac{-1}{5}\right)^nx^n \end{equation*}
Use differentiation and/or integration to express the following function as a power series (centered at \(x=0\)).
\begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{1}{(5+x)^2} \end{equation*}
