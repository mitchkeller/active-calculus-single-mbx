For example, the degree \(5\) Taylor approximation of \(f(x) = \ln(1+x)\) at \(a = 0\) is
\begin{equation*} T_5(x) = x - \frac{1}{2}x^2 + \frac{1}{3}x^3 - \frac{1}{4}x^4 + \frac{1}{5}x^5, \end{equation*}
and we see a pattern in the coefficients that allows us to easily generate \(T_6(x)\text{,}\) \(T_{10}(x)\text{,}\) or indeed \(T_n(x)\) for any \(n\text{.}\) Note that if we want to use \(T_{10}(x)\) to estimate \(\ln(\frac{3}{2}) = \ln(1 + \frac{1}{2})\text{,}\) we need to compute the sum of \(10\) terms given by
\begin{equation*} \ln\left( \frac{3}{2} \right) \approx T_{10}\left( \frac{1}{2} \right) = \left( \frac{1}{2} \right) - \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^2 + \frac{1}{3} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^3 - \frac{1}{4} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^4 + \cdots - \frac{1}{10} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{10}. \end{equation*}
This computation suggests at least two questions: is there an easy way (without a computer) to determine the exact value of the \(10\)-term sum
\begin{equation*} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right) - \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^2 + \frac{1}{3} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^3 - \frac{1}{4} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^4 + \cdots - \frac{1}{10} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{10}\text{,} \end{equation*}
and is there a way we can make sense of continuing the sum indefinitely,
\begin{equation*} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right) - \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^2 + \frac{1}{3} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^3 - \frac{1}{4} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^4 + \cdots - \frac{1}{10} \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{10} + \cdots? \end{equation*}
