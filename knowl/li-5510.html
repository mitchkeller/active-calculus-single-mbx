Item d.
For \(q(x) = x^2 \ln(1+x^4)\text{,}\) we start with the series for \(f(x) = \ln(1+x)\) and replace \(x\) by \(x^4\text{.}\) In particular, since
\begin{equation*} f(x) = \ln(1+x) = x - \frac{1}{2}x^2 + \frac{1}{3}x^3 - \cdots \end{equation*}
we have
\begin{equation*} f(x^4) = x^4 - \frac{1}{2}x^8 + \frac{1}{3}x^{12} - \cdots \end{equation*}
and thus
\begin{equation*} q(x) = x^2 f(x^4) = x^6 - \frac{1}{2}x^{10} + \frac{1}{3}x^{14} - \cdots\text{.} \end{equation*}
For the series for \(f(x^4)\) to converge, we need \(|x^4| \lt 1\) since the original series for \(f(x)\) converges for \(|x| \lt 1\text{.}\) Note that \(|x^4| \lt 1\) precisely when \(|x| \lt 1\text{.}\) Multiplying by \(x^2\) does not affect the values of \(x\) for which the series converges.
