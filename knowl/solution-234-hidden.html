The volume \(V\) of a cone of diameter \(d\) and height \(h\) is

\begin{equation*} V=\frac{\pi d^2 h }{12}. \end{equation*}

We might as well do this problem a little more generally than stated. (It actually makes things clearer to use variables throughout and substitute specific values only at the end.) So suppose the diameter of our tank is \(D\) and its height is \(H\text{.}\) Suppose the depth of the water is \(h\) and the radius of the surface is \(r\text{.}\) Then, by similar triangles,

\begin{equation*} \frac{r}{h} = \frac{R}{H}, \end{equation*}

or

\begin{equation*} r= \frac{Rh}{H}. \end{equation*}

Substituting in the volume formula gives

\begin{equation*} V= \frac{\pi D^2h^3}{12H^2}. \end{equation*}

Differentiating gives:

\begin{equation*} V'= \frac{3\pi D^2h^2h'}{12H^2} = \frac{\pi D^2h^2h'}{4H^2} \end{equation*}

This rate of change in volume would be the rate \(T\) at which water is being pumped into the tank if there was no leak. However, if \(L\) is the leak rate then

\begin{equation*} T = L+\frac{\pi D^2h^2h'}{4H^2}. \end{equation*}

Note that since we have to have consistent units of length, and the answer is in cubic centimeters per minute, the lengths given in meters have to be converted to centimeters.

Thus, substituting

\begin{equation*} L = 9600,\quad D = 500,\quad H = 700, \quad h = 150,\quad h' = 22 \end{equation*}

we obtain

\begin{equation*} T = 9600 + \frac{\pi*500^2*150^2*22}{4*700^2} \approx 207953 \hbox{cm}^3/\hbox{min}. \end{equation*}
