Solution 9.3.2.1.1.

The integral involves \(\tan u\) and \(\sec u\) where the exponent of \(\tan u\) is odd and positive. So we rewrite in order to have a copy of \(\sec(14 x) \tan(14 x)\) and use a trigonometric identity to turn the remaining even powers of \(\tan(14 x)\) into an expression of \(\sec^2(14 x)\text{.}\)
Then let \(u= \sec(14 x)\text{,}\) which means that \(du= 14 \sec(14 x)\tan(14 x) \, dx\text{,}\) and thus \(dx= \frac{du}{14\sec(14 x)\tan(14 x)}\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int 2\tan^{3}\!\left(14x\right)\sec^{3}\!\left(14x\right) \, dx \amp = 2 \int \tan^2(14 x) \sec^2(14 x) \sec(14 x) \tan(14 x) \, dx \\\\ \amp = 2 \int \bigg( \sec^2(14 x)-1 \bigg) \sec^2(14 x) \sec(14 x) \tan(14 x) \, dx \\\\ \amp = 2 \int \bigg( u^2-1 \bigg) u^2 \sec(14 x) \tan(14 x) \frac{du}{14\sec(14 x)\tan(14 x)} \\\\ \amp = \frac{2}{14} \int \bigg( u^2-1 \bigg) u^2 \, du \\\\ \amp = \frac{2}{14} \int \bigg( u^4-u^2 \bigg) \, du \\\\ \amp = \frac{2}{14} \bigg[\frac{ u^5}{5}-\frac{u^3}{3}\bigg]+C \\\\ \amp = \frac{2}{14} \bigg[\frac{ \sec^5(14 x)}{5}-\frac{\sec^3(14 x)}{3}\bigg]+C \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
The correct trigonometric identity used is C.
