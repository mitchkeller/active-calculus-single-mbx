Since \(\sin(x) = x - \frac{1}{3!}x^3 + \frac{1}{5!}x^7 - \cdots\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} \sin(1) = 1 - \frac{1}{3!}1^3 + \frac{1}{5!}1^7 - \frac{1}{7!}1^9 + \cdots \end{equation*}
so that, for example,
\begin{equation*} \sin(1) \approx 1 - \frac{1}{3!} + \frac{1}{5!} - \frac{1}{7!}\text{.} \end{equation*}
This approximation has error at most \(\frac{1}{9!}\) since that’s the next term in the sum. Since \(\frac{1}{9!} \approx 0.00000276\text{,}\) we are even more accurate than required. Note that since \(\frac{1}{7!} \approx 0.000198\text{,}\) we can’t stop after the third term and indeed need the four above. Thus,
\begin{equation*} \sin(1) \approx 1 - \frac{1}{3!} + \frac{1}{5!} - \frac{1}{7!} = \frac{4241}{5040} = 0.8414682539682539\ldots \end{equation*}
If we as a computational device the value of \(\sin(1)\text{,}\) we get \(\sin(1) = 0.84147098\ldots\text{.}\)
