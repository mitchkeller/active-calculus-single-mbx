First, \(f'(x) = \frac{3}{4}x^2\text{,}\) and thus \(f'(2) = 3\text{.}\) Next, \(g'(x) = \frac{1}{3}(4x-16)^{-2/3} \cdot 4\text{,}\) so that \(g'(6) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot 8^{-2/3} \cdot 4 = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{4} \cdot 4 = \frac{1}{3}\text{.}\) What is special about these two derivative values is that they happen to be reciprocals. Noting that \(f(2) = 3\) and \(g(3) = 2\text{,}\) we know that \(g'(3) = \frac{1}{f'(2)}\) by the rule for the derivative of an inverse function, and this matches our finding above that \(g'(3) = \frac{1}{3}\text{.}\)
