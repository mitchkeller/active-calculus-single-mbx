Solution 3.2.3.5.1.

\(f(x) = axe^{bx}\text{,}\) we have \(f'(x) = ae^{bx}+abxe^{bx}=ae^{bx}(1+bx)\text{.}\) We want \(f(\frac{1}{9})=1\text{,}\) and since this is to be a maximum, we require \(f'(\frac{1}{9})=0\text{.}\) These conditions give
\begin{equation*} f(1/9) = \frac{a}{9} e^{b/9} = 1 \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} f'(1/9) = a e^{b/9}(1+b/9) = 0. \end{equation*}
Since \(ae^{b/9}\) is non-zero, we can divide both sides of the second equation by \(ae^{b/9}\) to obtain \(1 + \frac{b}{9} = 0\text{.}\) This implies \(b=-9\text{.}\) Plugging \(b=-9\) into the first equation gives us \(a(\frac{1}{9})e^{-1}= 1\text{,}\) or \(a=9 e\text{.}\) How do we know we have a maximum at \(x=\frac{1}{9}\) and not a minimum? Since \(f'(x)=ae^{bx}(1+bx) = ()e^{-9 x}(1 - 9 x)\text{,}\) and \((9 e)e^{-9 x}\) is always positive, it follows that \(f'(x)>0\) when \(x\lt \frac{1}{9}\) and \(f'(x)\lt 0\) when \(x>\frac{1}{9}\text{.}\) Since \(f'\) is positive to the left of \(x=\frac{1}{9}\) and negative to the right of \(x = \frac{1}{9}\text{,}\) \(f(\frac{1}{9})\) is a local maximum.
