Solution 8.1.4.1.

We continue to work with \(f(x)=e^x\) and the four approximations of degree \(1\text{,}\) \(2\text{,}\) \(3\text{,}\) and \(4\) given by \(T_1(x)\text{,}\) \(T_2(x)\text{,}\) \(T_3(x)\text{,}\) and \(T_4(x)\text{.}\)

  2. The absolute error of the quadratic approximation at \(x = -1\) is \(|f(-1) - T_2(-1)| \approx 0.13212\text{,}\) and at \(x = 1\) is \(|f(1) - T_2(1)| \approx 0.21828\text{.}\)
  3. For the cubic approximation, \(T_3(x)\text{,}\) the absolute errors at \(x = -1\) and \(x = 1\) are \(|f(-1) - T_3(-1)| \approx 0.03455\) and \(|f(1) - T_3(1)| \approx 0.05162\text{.}\)
  4. For the quartic approximation, \(T_4(x)\text{,}\) the absolute errors at \(x = -1\) and \(x = 1\) are \(|f(-1) - T_4(-1)| \approx 0.00712\) and \(|f(1) - T_4(1)| \approx 0.00995\text{.}\)
  5. We see that as the degree of the approximation increases, the approximation improves significantly. For instance, just focusing on \(x = 1\text{,}\) moving from degree 2 to 3 to 4, the errors drop from about \(0.22\) to \(0.05\) to \(0.01\text{.}\) So at each fixed \(x\)-value, the approximation gets better, and in addition the interval of values on which the approximation is within a certain tolderance gets wider.
  6. If we widen the interval of \(x\)-values to something like \(-2 \lt x \lt 2\text{,}\) we see similar trends, but at the endpoints of the interval, there is a lot of error. For example, \(f(2) - T_2(2) \approx 2.39\text{;}\) even for \(T_4(x)\text{,}\) we have \(f(2) - T_4(2) \approx 0.39\text{.}\)
