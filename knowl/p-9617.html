For a series to be geometric, it must have a constant ratio between the terms. In \(T_f(x)\text{,}\) the general \(n\)th term is
\begin{equation*} a_n(x) = (-1)^{n-1}\frac{1}{n}x^n \end{equation*}
so the \((n+1)\)st term is
\begin{equation*} a_{n+1}(x) = (-1)^{n}\frac{1}{n+1}x^{n+1} \text{.} \end{equation*}
If we consider the ratio of these consecutive terms and call that quantity \(r_n(x)\text{,}\) we find that
\begin{equation*} r_n(x) = \frac{a_{n+1}(x)}{a_n(x)} = \frac{(-1)^{n}\frac{1}{n+1}x^{n+1}}{(-1)^{n-1}\frac{1}{n}x^n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
We can rewrite this fraction algebraically and simplify it considerably. Grouping the similar terms between the numerator and denominator, we see
\begin{equation*} r_n(x) = \frac{(-1)^{n}}{(-1)^{n-1}} \cdot \frac{n}{n+1} \cdot \frac{x^{n+1}}{x^n}\text{,} \end{equation*}
which allows us to simplify and conclude that
\begin{equation*} r_n(x) = (-1) \cdot \frac{n}{n+1} \cdot x\text{.} \end{equation*}
This tells us the series \(T_f(x)\) is not geometric since the ratio of consecutive terms depends on \(n\text{.}\) For example, if we take \(n = 2\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} r_2(x) = -\frac{2}{3}x\text{,} \end{equation*}
which is the ratio between the third and second terms of \(T_f(x)\text{,}\) while
\begin{equation*} r_3(x) = -\frac{3}{4}x \end{equation*}
is the ratio between the fourth and third terms. Since the ratio \(r_n(x)\) depends on \(n\text{,}\) the series lacks a common ratio between terms.
