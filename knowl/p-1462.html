  1. As seen at left in the figure below in part (d), \(g\) has a sharp corner point at \(x = 0\text{,}\) which makes \(g\) not differentiable at this point.
    By definition, we know
    \begin{align*} g'(0) & = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{g(0+h) - g(0)}{h}\\ & = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{g(h) - g(0)}{h}\\ & = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{|h|} - \sqrt{|0|}}{h}\\ & = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{|h|}}{h} \end{align*}
  2. In the following table, we record the values of \(\frac{\sqrt{|h|}}{h}\) for small values of \(h\text{.}\)
    \(h\) \(0.1\) \(0.01\) \(0.001\) \(0.0001\) \(-0.1\) \(-0.01\) \(-0.001\) \(-0.0001\)
    \(\sqrt{|h|}/h\) \(3.162\) \(10\) \(31.62\) \(100\) \(-3.162\) \(-10\) \(-31.62\) \(-100\)
    These results show from a different perspective that \(g'(0)\) does not exist. For small negative \(h\) values, the difference quotient is negative and getting more negative without bound. For small positive values of \(h\text{,}\) the difference quotient is positive and increasing without bound. This shows that \(\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{|h|}}{h}\) does not exist.
