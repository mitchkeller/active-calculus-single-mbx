Solution 8.5.4.1.

Let \(\erf(x) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}} \int_0^x e^{-t^2} \, dt \text{.}\)
  1. Since \(e^{x} = 1 + x + \frac{1}{2!}x^2 + \frac{1}{3!}x^3 + \cdots\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} e^{-t^2} = 1 - t^2 + \frac{1}{2!}t^4 - \frac{1}{3!}t^6 + \cdots \end{equation*}
  2. Now we evaluate the integral \(\int_0^x e^{-t^2} \, dt\) by replacing \(e^{-t^2}\) with its Taylor series:
    \begin{align*} \int_0^x e^{-t^2} \, dt = \mathstrut \amp \int_0^x 1 - t^2 + \frac{1}{2!}t^4 - \frac{1}{3!}t^6 + \cdots \, dt\\ = \mathstrut \amp \left. t - \frac{1}{3}t^3 + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!}t^5 - \frac{1}{7 \cdot 3!} t^7 + \cdots \right|_0^x\\ = \mathstrut \amp x - \frac{1}{3}x^3 + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!}x^5 - \frac{1}{7 \cdot 3!} x^7 + \cdots \end{align*}
  3. Since \(\erf(x) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}} \int_0^x e^{-t^2} \, dt\text{,}\) we now have
    \begin{equation*} \erf(x) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}} \left( x - \frac{1}{3}x^3 + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!}x^5 - \frac{1}{7 \cdot 3!} x^7 + \cdots \right) \end{equation*}
  4. This Taylor series will converge for every value of \(x\) since the original series for \(e^x\) converges for all real numbers.
  5. We can write \(\erf(0.5)\) as the infinite series of real numbers
    \begin{equation*} \erf(0.5) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}} \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3 \cdot 2^3} + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2! \cdot 2^5} - \frac{1}{7 \cdot 3! \cdot 2^7} + \cdots \right) \end{equation*}
    and approximate its value by truncating the series to a partial sum, such as
    \begin{equation*} \erf(0.5) \approx \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}} \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3 \cdot 2^3} + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2! \cdot 2^5} - \frac{1}{7 \cdot 3! \cdot 2^7} = 0.52049 \ldots\text{.} \end{equation*}
