Let’s now consider the definite integral
\begin{equation*} \int_0^1 e^{-x^2} \, dx\text{.} \end{equation*}
While we are unable to find an elementary algebraic antiderivative of \(e^{-x^2}\text{,}\) if we use the Taylor series
\begin{equation*} e^{-x^2} = 1 - x^2 + \frac{1}{2!}x^4 - \frac{1}{3!}x^6 + \cdots + (-1)^n \frac{1}{n!} x^{2n} + \cdots \end{equation*}
and apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to the series representation of \(e^{-x^2}\text{,}\) we find that
\begin{align} \int_0^1 e^{-x^2} \, dx =\mathstrut \amp \int_0^1 \left( 1 - x^2 + \frac{1}{2!}x^4 - \frac{1}{3!}x^6 + \cdots + (-1)^n \frac{1}{n!} x^{2n} + \cdots \right) \, dx\notag\\ =\mathstrut \amp \left. x - \frac{1}{3}x^3 + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!} x^5 + \cdots + \frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1) n!} x^{2n+1} + \cdots \right|_0^1 \notag\\ =\mathstrut \amp \left(1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!} + \cdots + \frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1) n!} + \cdots \right) - \left( 0 \right)\notag\\ =\mathstrut \amp 1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!} - \frac{1}{7 \cdot 3!} + \cdots + \frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1) n!} + \cdots\tag{8.5.4} \end{align}
The infinite series in Equation (8.5.4) is an example of an alternating series of real numbers. It turns out to be straightforward to determine whether or not an alternating series converges, and also to estimate the value of a convergent alternating series.
