Let \(u=\sec\!\left(2x\right)\text{.}\) Then we take the derivative of both sides to get
\begin{equation*} du = 2\sec\!\left(2x\right)\tan\!\left(2x\right) \, dx \end{equation*}
You can do the next step in different ways, but one way is to solve for \(dx\text{.}\) Since \(du = 2\sec\!\left(2x\right)\tan\!\left(2x\right) \, dx\text{,}\) we divide over to get that
\begin{equation*} dx=\frac{1}{2\sec\!\left(2x\right)\tan\!\left(2x\right)} \, du \end{equation*}
Then we substitute back into the original integral.
\begin{equation*} \int \frac{-3 \sec(2 x)\tan(2 x)}{\sec^{2}(2 x)} \, dx = \int \frac{-3 \sec(2 x)\tan(2 x)}{u^{2}} \frac{1}{2\sec\!\left(2x\right)\tan\!\left(2x\right)} \, du \end{equation*}
Which simplifies to
\begin{equation*} \int -\frac{1.5}{u^{2}}du \end{equation*}
Now, this is one of our basic integrals because we can write it as \(\displaystyle \int -1.5 u^{-2} \, du\) and thus we can integrate it. Constants in front just stay multiplied so we get
\begin{equation*} \int -1.5 u^{-2} \, du = \frac{-1.5}{-1} u^{-1}+C \end{equation*}
Finally, make sure you put it back in terms of the original variable, which in this case is 'x'. So we substitute back in for 'u', which was \(\sec\!\left(2x\right)\text{.}\) Thus the final answer to this integral is
\begin{equation*} \int \frac{-3 \sec(2 x)\tan(2 x)}{\sec^{2}(2 x)} \, dx = 1.5\!\left(\sec\!\left(2x\right)\right)^{-1}+C \end{equation*}
